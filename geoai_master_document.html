// What you're actually building

Your Mission

This is not a career plan. It is a life direction. Understand the difference — and why the distinction matters.

The framing that matters most: You are not building an "AgriTech portfolio." You are becoming an Earth Systems Engineer — someone who uses satellite data and spatial intelligence to monitor the physical systems that keep civilisation running: water, food, land, forests, oceans, and climate. AgriTech is where you start. The world is where you end up.
Why this domain specifically: The problems — drought, deforestation, flooding, food insecurity — exist on every continent. The organisations solving them deploy engineers to where the problems are. The same Sentinel-2 pipeline you're building in your bedroom today will run in Vietnam, Kenya, Peru, and Bangladesh. That portability is structural, not accidental.
Why you are protected from AI: Automation replaces tasks. It does not replace engineers who understand what the AI gets wrong, why it fails over irrigated fields, and how to build systems that decision-makers can trust and be accountable for. Build toward the judgment layer, not the execution layer. The plan below does exactly that.
The one principle to carry always: Go where the consequences of being wrong are large. Wrong flood maps cost lives. Wrong carbon estimates cost companies millions. Wrong drought forecasts delay food aid. In every one of these cases, a human expert remains essential. That is your long-term protection. Not a technology stack. Not a certificate. Genuine understanding of physical systems.

The Rules

These are not suggestions. These are the principles that separate the people who make it from the ones who don't.

01
Always know why the AI output is wrong
Every foundation model in GeoAI has known failure modes. Prithvi-EO-2.0 misclassifies irrigated fields. The engineer who can articulate these failures is irreplaceable. The engineer who trusts the output without understanding it is replaceable tomorrow.
02
Build domain vocabulary as seriously as your stack
Know what "evapotranspiration stress" means to an agronomist. Know what "return period" means to an insurance actuary. Know what "MRV compliance" means to an EU regulator. That translation layer is your long-term job security — not the Python libraries.
03
Use AI to accelerate, never to avoid understanding
Use Claude, Copilot, ChatGPT for boilerplate and debugging. Never to skip understanding what the code does. The moment you cannot explain your own pipeline without AI help, you have a gap that will show in an interview, a production failure, or a field deployment.
04
Specificity signals real work
"I built a general geospatial pipeline" is forgettable. "I built a crop health monitor for the Po Valley wheat belt that reveals the phenological cycle in NDVI time-series" is a sentence people remember. Be specific about your region, your problem, and what you found. Specificity is the signal of genuine depth.
05
Write publicly. Even a little.
One blog post on dev.to creates more networking conversations than six months of attending events. Engineers in this space read these. The people who will hire you for a FAO consultancy in three years will find you through your writing, not your LinkedIn profile.
06
Document everything that breaks
Every error — write it down. "Step X failed because Y, fixed by Z." That document is your blog post in three weeks. The interesting stories — the ones that make people lean in — come from hitting real problems and solving them, not from clean demos.
07
Read the Geospatial Jobs newsletter every week
geospatial.substack.com. Read every issue. It covers AgriTech, ClimateTech, risk modeling, energy, water, and remote sensing simultaneously. Pay attention to which companies are raising funding — they hire aggressively 3–6 months after a round. This is your market intelligence, not an optional extra.
08
Physical-world grounding is your permanent moat
AI is brilliant at pattern recognition in data. It is bad at dealing with the physical world's refusal to behave like training data. The engineer who understands the physical system — not just its data representation — will always have something to contribute that the model alone cannot provide. Protect this understanding. It is irreplaceable.
